# Culture and Etiquette

## Why It Matters

Small customs around greetings, dress, dining, and tipping vary widely
between cultures, and getting them roughly right goes a long way toward
being treated as a respectful guest rather than an obliviously loud
tourist. None of this is about rigid rules so much as paying attention to
what people around you are doing.

## Greetings and Personal Space

Handshakes are common but not universal; some cultures favor a slight bow,
a cheek kiss, or simply a verbal greeting without physical contact,
particularly between people who don't know each other well. When in doubt,
mirror the greeting offered rather than initiating a more familiar one.

## Dress Norms

Religious sites in many countries expect shoulders and knees covered
regardless of the visitor's own background, and some require head
coverings or shoe removal at the entrance. Carrying a light scarf or wrap
is an easy way to stay prepared without dressing conservatively for the
entire trip.

## Dining Customs

Tipping norms range from expected and significant, to a modest
round-up, to actively unwelcome depending on the country. Table manners
also vary: in some cultures finishing every bite on your plate signals
appreciation, in others leaving a small amount signals you were served
enough. Asking a local or checking ahead avoids sending the wrong signal
either way.

## Language Basics

Learning a handful of local phrases, greetings, "please," "thank you," and
"excuse me", is consistently one of the highest-value low-effort things a
traveler can do. It's less about fluency and more about signaling effort
and respect, which is often reciprocated with more patience and warmth
from locals.
