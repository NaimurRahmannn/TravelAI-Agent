# Safety and Health

## Before You Go

Check whether your destination recommends or requires any vaccinations, and
if so, get them early since some need multiple doses spaced weeks apart.
Register your trip with your home country's foreign travel advisory service
if one is available, so officials can reach you in an emergency. Buy travel
insurance that explicitly covers medical evacuation, not just trip
cancellation, since evacuation costs can be far higher than routine medical
care abroad.

## Everyday Precautions

Keep a digital and physical copy of emergency contacts, your accommodation
address, and the nearest embassy or consulate. Avoid displaying expensive
electronics or jewelry openly in unfamiliar areas, and use hotel safes for
passports and spare cash when not carrying them. Split payment cards and
cash across separate bags where practical, so a single lost or stolen bag
doesn't leave you stranded.

## Food and Water

In destinations where tap water isn't reliably safe to drink, stick to
sealed bottled water or water that has been boiled or filtered, and be
cautious with ice in drinks for the same reason. Freshly cooked, still-hot
food from busy stalls is generally safer than food that has been sitting
out, since high turnover means less time for bacteria to grow.

## During the Trip

Save the local emergency number (it isn't the same everywhere) and know
the location of the nearest hospital or clinic to your accommodation.
Trust your instincts about unfamiliar situations, and let someone know your
plans if heading somewhere remote, including an expected return time.

## Health Documentation

Carry a summary of any chronic conditions, allergies, and current
medications (including generic drug names, since brand names vary by
country) in case you need care from a provider unfamiliar with your
history.
